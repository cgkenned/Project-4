On Thursday, July 13, 2017, U.S. Secretary of Education Betsy DeVos met with The National Coalition for Men and Stop Abusive and Violent Environments, both of which call themselves advocates for "men's rights." DeVos met with these groups in what she said was an effort to equally weigh the claims of university sexual assault victims and the due-process rights of the accused.

This came one day after Candice Jackson, DeVos' assistant secretary for civil rights, told The New York Times that campus sexual assault "accusations — 90 percent of them — fall into the category of, 'We were both drunk, we broke up, and six months later I found myself under a Title IX investigation because she just decided that our last sleeping together was not quite right.'" Jackson later apologized for the remark, calling it "flippant."

According to The Campus Sexual Assault Study conducted by the U.S. Department of Justice in 2007, less than 5% of sexual assault survivors on college campuses report the incident to a law enforcement agency. Of those who did not report the incident to law enforcement, the most frequently cited reason for not reporting was that survivors believed the event would not be considered serious enough by authorities.
